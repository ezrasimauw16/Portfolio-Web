INFORMATICS STUDENT · UNSRAT

Ezra Steve Simauw.

I've always been an artist at heart. Long before I ever touched a line of code, my world was all about the scratch of a pencil on paper and whatever melodies I could pick out on the piano or guitar. To be honest, my path hasn't exactly been a straight line. When I had to choose between a "starving artist" hobby and a steady career, I chose the future: Informatics at UNSRAT. I stepped into the world of technical logic not because I lost my passion for drawing, but because I wanted to see if I could build a bridge between the two.

That choice has made me a "flexible" creator in the best way possible. My toolkit is pretty broad — I'm a singer, a dancer, and a musician; a photographer, a graphic designer, and a video editor. Drawing was my first love, but I've found that all these different skills actually feed into each other. My musical taste — everything from the precision of Michael Jackson to the harmonies of the Backstreet Boys — gives my visual work a certain rhythm. I don't just look at a design or a photo; I'm looking for the "groove" in the composition.

People often ask how an Informatics student ends up so deep in the arts. For me, it's simple: Informatics is the skeleton, and art is the soul. My technical studies have given me a meticulous, logical eye that a lot of "pure" artists might miss. When I'm coding, I'm building a system; when I'm framing a shot or designing a layout, I'm using that same logic to find the perfect balance. Understanding how the web works "under the hood" gives me an edge in making digital experiences that actually feel right.

At the end of the day, I don't really believe in sticking to just one box. I'm a multidisciplinary creator who believes that logic and creativity are just two sides of the same coin. Whether I'm writing code or working on a new design, my goal is to stay adaptable and keep things fresh. I'm here to prove that you don't have to trade your artistic nature for a steady career — you just have to be flexible enough to let the two worlds collide.
